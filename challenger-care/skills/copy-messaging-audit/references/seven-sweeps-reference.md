# Seven Sweeps — Full Reference with Adaptations

## Origins

The Seven Sweeps methodology was developed by Joanna Wiebe of Copyhackers, the leading copy-for-conversion resource. The sweeps were designed for iterative copy auditing: run each sweep independently so you're looking for one specific class of problem at a time, rather than trying to catch everything simultaneously.

has adapted the framework to:
1. Integrate with the CRO research pipeline (VoC, heatmap, heuristic findings feed directly into the sweeps)
2. Tie each finding to a testable CRO hypothesis
3. Calibrate to Challenger mix (lead-gen B2B, lead-gen B2C, eComm DTC)

## Sweep 1 — Clarity: Worked Examples

### Before / After: Vague headline

❌ **Before:** "Your Business. Our Expertise."
→ Says nothing about what the company does or who it helps

✅ **After:** "All-Inclusive Small Business Workspace — Month-to-Month, No Overhead"
→ States what it is, who it's for, and the key differentiator

### Before / After: Weak CTA

❌ **Before:** "Learn More" → Where am I going? What will I find?

✅ **After:** "See Available Spaces Near You" → I know exactly what happens next

### Clarity diagnostic questions
1. If I showed this headline to a stranger, could they tell me what this company sells? ✓/✗
2. Is the primary CTA action clear without reading the body copy? ✓/✗
3. Is there a "who this is for" signal above the fold? ✓/✗
4. Does the page have one clear primary action, or is it competing with itself? ✓/✗

## Sweep 2 — Voice and Tone: Worked Examples

### Red flags

**Passive voice hiding accountability:**
❌ "Results may vary based on individual circumstances"
✅ "Most clients see a 15–25% reduction in fees in the first 90 days"

**Corporate-speak in a friendly brand:**
❌ "We leverage our extensive network of strategic partnerships to deliver optimized outcomes"
✅ "We find you the best rate because we work with 50+ processors — not just one"

**Generic adjectives:**
❌ "Our seamless, innovative platform delivers best-in-class solutions"
✅ "Set up in 10 minutes, no coding required"

### Voice calibration for clients

| Client type | Voice standard |
|-------------|---------------|
| B2B Commercial Real Estate (RISE) | Professional but warm. Direct. "We" and "you" language. Specific, not corporate. |
| B2C Payment Processing (NBC) | Plain-spoken, confident, slightly combative toward incumbents (Square, Stripe). No jargon. |
| eComm DTC | Depends on brand identity — but always: customer-centric, benefit-forward, brand-specific vocabulary |

## Sweep 3 — The "So What?" : Worked Examples

### Feature → Benefit translation examples

| Feature (what they wrote) | "So what?" | Benefit copy |
|--------------------------|-----------|-------------|
| "All-inclusive amenities" | So what does that mean? | "Utilities, WiFi, parking, and on-site management included — so you only pay rent" |
| "24/7 security" | So what does that mean for me? | "Your inventory and equipment are protected around the clock, even when you're not there" |
| "Flexible lease terms" | So what? | "Move in on your timeline and scale up (or out) as your business grows — no 5-year lock-in" |
| "ISO 27001 certified" | So what? | "Your data is secured to the same standard as financial institutions" |

### "So What?" test

Read each feature claim and ask aloud: "So what?" Keep asking until you reach an outcome the customer cares about. That final answer is the benefit copy.

## Sweep 4 — Proof: The proof hierarchy in action

### Level 1 — Specific third-party data with source
"Processing 2.6% per transaction with Square costs the average small business $3,200/year in fees. (Source: Square fee calculator, 2025)"

### Level 2 — Named case study with quantified result
"Kerrigan Roofing moved to RISE Richfield in March 2024 and cut monthly overhead by $1,800 while adding 40% more warehouse space."

### Level 3 — Named testimonial with specifics
"'When I tell you this place saved my business — I moved from a place that just raised rent 30% and couldn't give me any support. RISE is completely different.' — Aaron Gottesman, Kerrigan Roofing"

### Level 4 — Star rating with review count from recognized platform
"4.9 stars · 340+ Google reviews"

### Proof audit checklist

For each major claim on the page:
- [ ] Is it backed by Proof Level 1–4?
- [ ] Are testimonials specific (name, company, outcome)?
- [ ] Are statistics sourced and dated?
- [ ] Are review counts from verifiable platforms?
- [ ] Are claims competitors can also make flagged for differentiation?

## Sweep 5 — Specificity: Worked Examples

### Generic → Specific transformations

❌ "We serve businesses nationwide"
✅ "340+ businesses across Indiana, Ohio, Minnesota, and Wisconsin call RISE home"

❌ "Save time and money"
✅ "Most clients save 47 minutes per day and $800/month switching from Square"

❌ "Quick setup"
✅ "Processing in 24 hours — guaranteed"

❌ "Our team has extensive experience"
✅ "Our team has processed over $2 billion in transactions across 12 years"

### The specificity rule
When you can replace a vague number with a specific one without making it up, do it. Specific numbers (47 minutes, $800/month, 340+ businesses) are more credible than round numbers because they feel earned rather than estimated.

## Sweep 6 — Heightened Emotion: Worked Examples

### Pain-first opening (before → after)

❌ "RISE Commercial District offers flexible workspace solutions for growing businesses."
✅ "Most small businesses spend their first years fighting for space, overpaying for overhead, and dealing with landlords who don't care. There's a better way to run your business."

### Loss-framing example

❌ "Get a free rate analysis"
✅ "Find out how much Square is overcharging you — get a free rate analysis"

### Aspiration example (eComm)

❌ "Premium quality leather goods for everyday use"
✅ "The bag you'll still be carrying in 10 years"

### Empathy signal examples

For B2B / lead-gen:
- "If you're tired of fee surprises on your monthly statement..."
- "Most business owners come to us after being locked into a 3-year contract they can't afford to break"
- "You shouldn't need a PhD in payment processing to know if you're being charged fairly"

For eComm DTC:
- "We made [product] because we couldn't find one that actually [solved problem]"
- "Built for people who [specific situation] and need [specific outcome]"

## Sweep 7 — Zero Risk: Full Checklist

### Hesitation audit — near each CTA, check:

**Commitment hesitation** (is this a big ask?):
- [ ] Does the CTA communicate low commitment? ("Free quote," "No obligation," "Cancel anytime")
- [ ] Is the form length appropriate? (3–5 fields max for first-touch forms)
- [ ] Is "speak to sales" the primary CTA? (Replace with lower-friction option when possible)

**Privacy hesitation** (will they spam me?):
- [ ] Is there a privacy statement near the form? ("We never share your information")
- [ ] Is the phone field labeled clearly? ("We'll only call to discuss your quote")

**Quality hesitation** (is this legit?):
- [ ] Is there a trust signal immediately adjacent to the CTA? (rating, client count, or named testimonial)
- [ ] Are there visible security/trust badges if payment info is collected?

**Process hesitation** (what happens after I click?):
- [ ] Does the CTA set expectations for what happens next? ("We'll respond within 24 hours")
- [ ] Is there a "what to expect" step or timeline below the form?

## Applying the sweeps to client types

### Lead-gen B2C (NBC-style)

Priority sweeps: 1 (Clarity) → 4 (Proof) → 7 (Zero Risk)
- Clarity: The form must tell users exactly what they're getting
- Proof: Claims about "lower rates" and "better service" need specific data
- Zero Risk: CPL forms have high abandonment — reduce friction at every step

Primary test types generated: CTA copy tests, trust signal placement tests, form field reduction tests, messaging angle tests

### Lead-gen B2B (RISE-style)

Priority sweeps: 3 (So What?) → 5 (Specificity) → 6 (Heightened Emotion)
- So What: Features like "all-inclusive" and "flexible leases" need benefit bridges
- Specificity: Location coverage, business count, and space types need to be specific
- Heightened Emotion: Commercial real estate is a high-stakes decision — copy must acknowledge the pain

Primary test types generated: Headline messaging tests, social proof format tests, CTA copy tests, hero copy tests

### eComm DTC

Priority sweeps: 2 (Voice/Tone) → 3 (So What?) → 6 (Heightened Emotion)
- Voice/Tone: DTC brands live and die by authentic, specific brand voice
- So What: Product features need to connect to identity and lifestyle outcomes
- Heightened Emotion: Purchase decisions are emotional; copy must sell the feeling

Primary test types generated: Headline tests, product description copy tests, trust signal tests, above-fold copy tests
