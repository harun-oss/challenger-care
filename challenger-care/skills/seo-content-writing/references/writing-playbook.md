# SEO Content Writing Playbook

## Intro Formulas by Intent Type

The intro is the highest-churn section of any article. Readers make a "is this worth my time" decision in the first 3–4 sentences. The wrong opener (a keyword definition, "In today's digital landscape...", a generic statement about importance) signals to the reader — and to Google's quality systems — that this is low-value content.

### Formula 1 — Problem-First (Informational guides)

```
[Specific, vivid description of the problem the reader is experiencing]
[Why the conventional approach fails / why this is harder than it looks]
[What this article gives them — specific, not generic]
```

**Example for "how to reduce customer churn":**
> Every month, a portion of your customer base quietly cancels. Some leave feedback. Most don't. If your churn rate is above 5% annually in SaaS, you're spending more on acquisition than you're retaining — a treadmill that gets harder to run. This guide covers the specific interventions that move churn numbers: not the generic advice, but the mechanisms that explain why customers leave and how to address each.

**What NOT to do:**
> "Customer churn is one of the most important metrics for any SaaS business. In this article, we will discuss the best strategies to reduce churn and improve retention."

The bad version defines a keyword and announces the article's existence. The good version earns the read.

### Formula 2 — Contrast-First (Comparison / Roundup)

```
[State the obvious choice / conventional wisdom]
[Complicate it — why the obvious choice isn't always right]
[Your angle: what this comparison actually resolves]
```

**Example for "Klaviyo vs Salesforce":**
> Klaviyo gets recommended to startups. Salesforce gets recommended to enterprises. But the line between "startup" and "enterprise" has blurred — and both products have evolved far beyond their original lanes. This comparison cuts through the positioning to answer the question that actually matters: at what point does Klaviyo's ease-of-use advantage become Salesforce's scalability advantage, and how do you know which side of that line you're on?

### Formula 3 — Stakes-First (Transactional / Service pages)

```
[What's at stake if they choose wrong]
[What the ideal outcome looks like]
[Why this page / this service is the specific path to that outcome]
```

**Example for "CRO agency for Shopify":**
> The average Shopify store converts at 1.4%. The top quartile converts at 3.2%. That gap isn't about product or pricing — it's about what happens between click and checkout. If you're spending on acquisition to a store that converts at 1.4%, you're funding a leaky bucket. This is what a focused CRO engagement addresses: the specific friction points that separate your store from the stores converting at twice your rate.

### Formula 4 — Data-First (Research / Statistics-based content)

```
[Surprising or counterintuitive statistic]
[Why it matters / what it implies]
[What this article unpacks about it]
```

**Example for "AI Overviews impact on SEO":**
> Organic CTR dropped 61% on queries where an AI Overview appears above the results. But for brands cited inside those AI Overviews, clicks increased 35% compared to ranking #1 without citation. The same feature that's cannibalising organic traffic is also creating an outsized visibility opportunity — for the brands that understand how to get cited.

## E-E-A-T Integration Patterns — Worked Examples

### Experience — showing first-hand knowledge without sounding like a case study

**Weak (generic, no experience):**
> Many businesses find that personalisation improves email open rates. Studies show that personalised emails can increase open rates by 26%.

**Strong (first-hand experience woven in):**
> When we ran a personalisation audit for a 7-figure DTC client last quarter, their list was split almost 50/50 between engaged and lapsed. Sending the same subject line to both segments produced a 2.1% average open rate — but once we built separate flows for each segment, the engaged segment hit 38% opens and the lapsed segment hit 12%. The average hid both the win and the problem. Personalisation doesn't just improve opens — it makes your reporting actually useful.

The strong version includes: specific timeframe ("last quarter"), specific metric (50/50 split, exact open rates), and a counterintuitive insight (averaging hides the real story). This is information only someone who ran the experiment would know.

### Expertise — field-accurate language vs generic treatment

**Weak (non-expert language):**
> To make your website faster, you should compress your images and remove unnecessary code.

**Strong (field-accurate, specific):**
> LCP above 4 seconds is almost always either a TTFB problem (server response above 600ms, usually caused by an app making a server-side request) or an image problem (uncompressed, wrong format, or lazy-loaded above the fold). Run the URL through PageSpeed Insights first: it will identify the LCP element. If TTFB is the bottleneck, image optimisation won't help — you need to identify which installed app is making the slow request.

The expert version uses precise terminology, explains causality, and gives a specific diagnostic path. A non-expert could not write it.

### Authoritativeness — citing primary sources, not secondary summaries

**Weak (secondary citation, vague):**
> According to recent studies, AI is impacting search significantly.

**Strong (primary source, specific):**
> Google's March 2024 announcement confirmed that INP (Interaction to Next Paint) replaced FID as a Core Web Vitals metric. Unlike FID, which measured only the first interaction, INP measures all interactions throughout the page visit — making it significantly more demanding for pages with app-heavy JavaScript.

Link directly to the Google announcement, not to a blog post summarising the announcement.

### Trustworthiness — acknowledging nuance and limitations

**Weak (overconfident, no caveats):**
> Using this strategy will dramatically improve your rankings.

**Strong (honest, qualified):**
> This approach has improved rankings in 7 of the 9 sites we've applied it to — but the exceptions matter. Sites in highly competitive niches with fewer than 20 external backlinks saw minimal movement in the first 90 days. The tactic is a quality signal, not a shortcut past authority gaps.

Acknowledging when something doesn't work — and under what conditions — makes the cases where it does work more credible.

## 2024–2025 Helpful Content System Checklist

The Helpful Content System (now integrated into Google's core algorithm as of March 2024) penalises content written primarily for search engines rather than readers. Use this checklist before publishing any page.

### The 18 self-assessment questions (adapted from Google's guidance)

**Original purpose:**
- [ ] Is this content primarily written to help people, or primarily written to rank in search?
- [ ] Does the content demonstrate clear first-hand expertise and depth of knowledge?
- [ ] Is there a reason a reader would trust this content beyond it appearing in search results?
- [ ] Would the site be considered authoritative on this topic by someone who knows the subject?
- [ ] Is the content free of factual errors that an expert would catch?

**Depth and quality:**
- [ ] Does the content provide substantial value beyond what's available on other pages for this query?
- [ ] Does it provide original information, analysis, research, or synthesis?
- [ ] Does it provide more than what's needed to satisfy the search query (i.e., padding)?
- [ ] Is every section present because it helps the reader, not because it targets an additional keyword?
- [ ] Are headings and titles descriptive, not clickbait?

**Production quality:**
- [ ] Is the content well-edited? (Spelling, grammar, formatting)
- [ ] Is it produced with care — images properly labelled, sources cited, dates visible?
- [ ] Does it work well on mobile?
- [ ] Does the page load in a reasonable time?

**User signals:**
- [ ] Would a user who lands on this page feel satisfied, or would they return to Google for a better answer?
- [ ] Is there a clear next step or CTA that helps the user take action?
- [ ] Does the content avoid making the user feel they need to search again to find the full answer?
- [ ] Is the content free of intrusive ads or interstitials that would frustrate a reader?

**High-risk signals (any "yes" here = rewrite or remove):**
- [ ] Was this content primarily generated by AI without human editorial review?
- [ ] Does the site have pages across many unrelated topics with no clear focus or expertise area?
- [ ] Is there an excessive amount of content on your site targeting a large number of search queries without clear value differentiation between pages?

## SaaS Landing Page Copy Framework

### The core structure

```
ABOVE THE FOLD
H1: [What you do — for whom — what outcome they get]
Subhead: [Second benefit or mechanism — adds specificity]
CTA: [One action — free trial / book demo / see pricing]
Social proof: [Number of customers / logos / notable testimonial]

PROBLEM SECTION
H2: [The problem in the customer's language — not your solution yet]
  Body: Describe the problem viscerally. Use the customer's language from reviews/interviews.
  Specifics: Numbers, before/after, what failure costs them
  Transition: "There's a better way."

SOLUTION SECTION  
H2: [Your product name] [key benefit]
  Body: How the product solves the problem — feature by feature, each framed as an outcome
  Format: Benefit → Feature → Evidence (not Feature → Benefit)
  Visual: Screenshot, demo, or short video

USE CASE / WHO IT'S FOR SECTION
H2: Built for [specific customer type]
  Body: 2–3 specific use cases with outcomes
  Specificity: "If you're a [role] dealing with [problem], here's what changes..."

SOCIAL PROOF SECTION
H2: What [customers] say
  Format: Named testimonial + company + specific outcome (not generic praise)
  Add: Case study link if available
  Data: "Average customer sees X in Y time"

PRICING / HOW TO GET STARTED
H2: Pricing (or "Simple Pricing")
  Be transparent — pricing pages that hide prices frustrate evaluators and increase churn later
  Free tier / trial / money-back guarantee: state clearly

FAQ SECTION
H2: Common Questions
  Source: Address the top 3–5 sales objections in FAQ format
  Schema: FAQPage applied

FINAL CTA
  Repeat the primary action
  Add secondary CTA for not-yet-ready users (e.g., "See a demo" if primary is "Free trial")
```

### Benefit vs Feature framing

| Feature-first (weak) | Benefit-first (strong) |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "We have an AI-powered dashboard" | "See which pages are losing traffic before it shows up in your revenue" |
| "Multi-user collaboration" | "Your whole team works in the same tool — no more spreadsheet versions or Slack threads to reconcile" |
| "API integrations" | "Connects to your existing stack in an afternoon — no dev sprint required" |
| "Custom reporting" | "Build the report your CFO asks for every quarter — without exporting to Excel" |

The rule: features are inputs, benefits are outputs. Buyers care about outputs.

## Comparison Page Structure Template

Comparison pages are the highest-converting organic content type for SaaS. A "[Competitor] alternative" or "[Brand] vs [Competitor]" page captures users who are already in the decision phase — they just need to decide between specific options.

### Honest framing principle

The biggest mistake in competitor comparison pages is obvious bias. Readers who already know the competitor product will immediately detect inaccurate or one-sided coverage and leave. The paradox: the more honestly you represent the competitor's strengths, the more credible your brand's strengths become.

**Example of biased, low-trust framing:**
> "Klaviyo is complicated and overpriced. Our tool does everything better for half the price."

**Example of honest, high-trust framing:**
> "Klaviyo's CMS and marketing suite are among the most mature in the category. If you need a comprehensive all-in-one with extensive third-party integrations and don't mind the learning curve or price point, Klaviyo is a strong choice. Where [Brand] consistently wins: implementation time, pricing at the SMB tier, and hands-on onboarding. Here's what that means in practice."

### Structural notes for maximum conversion

1. **Skimmable summary first** — many readers have already decided; confirm it for them
2. **Comparison table** — structured data helps AI citation AND gives skimmers what they need
3. **Acknowledge competitor strengths** — the trust-building move
4. **"Who should choose X"** sections — explicit segmentation reduces friction at decision point
5. **Price transparency** — any vagueness about pricing on your side creates doubt
6. **CTA timing** — place a CTA after the comparison table (when readers have just seen the win) and again at the end

### Keeping comparison pages current

Competitor products change. Prices change. Features change. A comparison page with outdated information is worse than no comparison page — it signals that your company doesn't pay attention.

Maintenance protocol:
- Set a calendar reminder to audit every comparison page quarterly
- Subscribe to competitor changelog/release notes
- Update the `dateModified` schema field and the visible "last updated" date with each revision
- Monitor the page's ranking: comparison pages that age without updates typically lose rankings within 12–18 months
