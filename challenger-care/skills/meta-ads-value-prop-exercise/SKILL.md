# Meta Ads Value Prop Exercise

**Owner:** AM / the operator (joint — requires brand knowledge from AM and research from the operator)
**Timeline:** Week 2 — kickoff deliverable #4 (may extend to Week 3 for complex brands)
**Outcome:** Complete Value Prop Exercise deck delivered to client — covering brand positioning, customer personas, pain points, emotional drivers, unique differentiators, and ad messaging angles.

> This is the most research-intensive of the 5 kickoff deliverables. Unlike the other decks, the entire document needs to be completed — not just the first slide. Use AI (Claude, ChatGPT) actively to accelerate research; the SOP explicitly recommends this.

---

## Phase 1: Context & Research Inputs

Ask AM / the operator:
1. **Client name and URL** — website to research
2. **Products or services** — what do they sell, at what price point?
3. **Target customer** — who do they believe their customer is? (demographics, interests, job role for B2B)
4. **Existing materials** — do you have access to any of the following?
   - Customer reviews (Amazon, Trustpilot, Google, Shopify reviews)
   - Previous ad copy or creative that performed well
   - The Client Workbook (any positioning already documented)
   - Any customer surveys or VoC data
5. **Biggest competitors** — 2–3 competitors they mention most often

> Existing reviews are gold. Real customer language — the exact words people use to describe the product and their problems — is the most valuable input for this exercise. If you have access to 20+ reviews, start there before doing any other research.

---

## Phase 2: Brand Research

Research Challenger's brand across these dimensions. Use Claude/ChatGPT to accelerate — prompt it with the website URL and ask it to identify positioning, tone, and differentiators.

> For the full research framework and AI prompt templates for each section, read `references/value-prop-research-guide.md`.

### 2A: Product / Service Analysis
- What does the product actually do? (functional benefit)
- What problem does it solve?
- What is the price point — and what does that signal about the target customer?
- Are there different tiers, bundles, or subscription options?

### 2B: Brand Positioning
- What tone does the brand use? (premium, friendly, clinical, bold, minimal?)
- What do they lead with on their homepage? (product features, outcomes, lifestyle, social proof?)
- Do they have a clear tagline or brand promise?
- What visual style does the brand use? (photography vs. illustration, aspirational vs. relatable)

### 2C: Customer Reviews Mining
Pull 20–30 reviews from wherever available. Look for patterns in:
- **What customers love most** (repeat themes = core value props)
- **Problems they had before buying** (these become pain points in ads)
- **Language they use** (direct quotes often become headline copy)
- **Objections or complaints** (what hesitations do customers mention?)

Structure your findings:
```
Top customer love themes:
1. [Theme + example quote]
2. [Theme + example quote]
3. [Theme + example quote]

Top pain points mentioned:
1. [Pain point]
2. [Pain point]

Objections / hesitations:
1. [Objection]
2. [Objection]
```

## Phase 3: Customer Persona Development

Define 2–3 customer personas. Each persona should be grounded in real signals (reviews, website copy, client input) — not invented.

For each persona:

```
Persona Name: [Give them a descriptive name, e.g., "The Overwhelmed Parent"]
Demographics: [Age range, gender if relevant, income level, location]
Job/Role: [Relevant for B2B; lifestyle descriptor for B2C]

Core Problem: [The problem they have that this product solves]
What They've Tried Before: [Previous solutions they've used — why didn't those work?]
What They Want: [Desired outcome, not just the product feature]
Emotional Driver: [The deeper emotional reason behind the purchase — e.g., peace of mind, status, belonging]
Biggest Objection: [Why they might NOT buy — price, skepticism, better alternatives]

Best Ad Angles for This Persona:
- [Angle 1: Problem-aware → solution]
- [Angle 2: Outcome/aspiration]
- [Angle 3: Social proof / credibility]
```

## Phase 4: Unique Differentiators

Define what genuinely separates this brand from competitors. This is distinct from what the brand *thinks* differentiates it — focus on what customers actually respond to.

Ask:
- If I removed the brand name from this product description, would it sound like any competitor? If yes, it's not a differentiator.
- What do reviewers say that they can't get elsewhere?
- What does the brand do that is genuinely harder to copy? (proprietary formula, unique sourcing, founder story, community, price-to-quality ratio)

Structure differentiators into a table:

| Differentiator | Evidence (reviews, copy, data) | Ad Angle |
|---|---|---|
| [Differentiator 1] | [Source] | [How to use in ad] |
| [Differentiator 2] | [Source] | [How to use in ad] |
| [Differentiator 3] | [Source] | [How to use in ad] |

## Phase 5: Messaging Angles

Translate the research into 5–6 distinct ad messaging angles. Each angle should be testable — meaning it represents a different psychological appeal or audience segment.

> For angle frameworks and examples by vertical, read `references/messaging-angles-framework.md`.

Common Meta ad angles that perform well:

| Angle Type | When to Use | Example Hook |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem → Solution** | Aware of problem, unaware of product | "Tired of X? There's a better way." |
| **Social Proof** | Skeptical buyers, high-ticket | "10,000 customers trust [Brand] for X" |
| **Before/After** | Transformation products (fitness, beauty, software) | "Before: [problem]. After: [outcome]." |
| **Specificity/Data** | B2B, high-consideration | "Reduce [pain point] by 40% in 30 days" |
| **Identity/Tribe** | Lifestyle or community products | "For people who [identity statement]" |
| **Objection Handling** | High-ticket, skeptical | "You don't need to [objection]. Here's why." |

For each angle, write a sample headline (3–5 words) and a one-line description of who it's for.

## Phase 6: Synthesis Brief

Before building the final deliverable, write a brief summary of key findings. This is used by the orchestrator to carry findings forward to the next skill in a multi-skill workflow.

**[Client Name] Value Prop Exercise Key Findings**
- Top finding 1 — specific value prop with supporting data. Example: "Core value prop: time savings + simplicity. 60% of reviews mention frustration with competitors' complexity"
- Top finding 2 — specific persona insight with emotional driver. Example: "Primary customer persona: overwhelmed mid-level manager, age 30–45, seeking efficiency gains. Emotional driver is confidence/control"
- Top finding 3 — specific differentiator with supporting evidence. Example: "Unique differentiator: 10x faster setup than competitors. Supported by review data and founder story"

**Priority for downstream skills:** What the next skill should focus on — be specific to the creative workflow. Example: "Next skill should use the simplicity and speed differentiators as the primary messaging angles. Emphasize confidence and control in creative copy. Personas show low technical depth, so avoid jargon in ads."

*If running standalone (not in an orchestrated chain), share this summary with the operator before the full deliverable.*

>
> 1. **DOCX Report** — full written value prop document with all 5 sections: brand analysis, review mining, personas, differentiators, and messaging angles. Best for internal strategy use and client handoff.
> 2. **Deck brief** — structured notes to populate the [Value Prop Exercise template](https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1qQXitcyzppzWYnhUG22Ju8C67RX68s9pm1qcUhBZCYk/edit) section by section
> 3. **Messaging angles summary** — just the angles and headline hooks, for immediate use in ad copy development"

> "branding, or client-specific? If Challenger, I'll pull from the brand file. If client-specific, share their colours and logo."

If branding: read `../../../growthit-brand/assets/growthit-brand.md` for exact colours, fonts, and layout rules before building output.

Build based on chosen format:
- **DOCX** → invoke the docx skill; apply branding; structure as: brand analysis → review mining → personas → differentiators → messaging angles
- **Deck brief** → section-by-section notes mapped to the template slide order
- **Messaging angles** → a clean table of angles, headlines, and target personas
